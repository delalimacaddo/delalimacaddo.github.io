How Canadian Universities Represent Postgraduate Opportunities for West Africa

Every year, 122,000 students apply to study in Canada. Behind each application is a carefully crafted story about who belongs and what's possible.

By Delali Mac-Addo

6 min read

Introduction

Millions of young people scroll through posts shared by universities — stories of campus life, graduations, and student achievements. These posts don't just document success. They shape imagination.

Picture this: You're 25, scrolling Instagram. You see someone who feels familiar — whose roots, identity, or culture mirror your own. They're wearing a familiar outfit (kente cloth) at graduation. They're from Ondo State, Nigeria — maybe even the same place as your cousin. They've started a business that's thriving and solving real problems. They embody your idea of a successful career.

The caption says: "Discover how the university helped make it happen."

That post isn't random. It's part of a larger pattern in how postgraduate opportunity is represented.

Between January and September 2024, Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada processed nearly 122,000 study permit applications from Cameroon, Côte d'Ivoire, Ghana, Guinea, and Nigeria. Each one represents a dream and a vision of the future.

But here's what we don't usually ask: How do universities shape that vision? What makes a 25-year-old in West Africa believe that studying in Canada is not just possible, but meant for them and tied to their career ambitions?

I spent months analyzing the Instagram feeds, website pages, LinkedIn posts, and YouTube videos of two major Canadian universities. I examined what they post, who they feature, and how they tell stories about postgraduate opportunity.

What I found wasn't random. It was a system. A three-layer narrative structure that appears again and again, post after post, story after story.

Layer 1: They show you someone who shares similar identities to yours.
Layer 2: They show you what that person achieved.
Layer 3: They tell you where it happened.

This is how opportunity is represented. Not through facts or statistics or program descriptions. Through Identity. Achievement. Affiliation.

And it works because it taps into something deeper than logic. It shapes imagination itself — what you believe is possible, who you see yourself becoming, and where you think you belong.

Someone Like You

"I see myself in this."

What you just scrolled through tells you something. Post after post, African students and alumni are featured telling their stories in their own voices — each one anchored by names, home cities, and cultural markers.

This is not incidental. It is a representational choice.

By highlighting these elements, universities make African identities visible within the Canadian campus context. The content repeatedly links specific cities, cultures, and national identities to the institution itself. Geographic distance is not ignored. It is addressed visually.

Familiarity plays a role here — language that resonates, faces that reflect shared backgrounds, stories told in recognizable ways. What might feel abstract or distant begins to feel tangible. The space becomes imaginable. Attainable.

The message is simple: "Someone like me has already done this."

Recognition comes first. Before you even think about applying, you need to believe this pathway is meant for people like you.

Look What's Possible

"This could be my story."

Once you see yourself in the story, the universities show you what's possible.

Not with vague promises. With proof.

A Nigerian graduate named Olugbenga launches Reeddi, a clean-energy company. The number is right there in the post: 3,000+ households and businesses in Nigeria now have electricity because of his work.

Faith, from Ondo State, graduates with a psychology degree. But the post doesn't stop there. It shows her building a photography business. Getting involved in community service. Moving into postgraduate study.

These aren't statistics. They're people. With names. With faces. With measurable results.

Opportunity stops being abstract. It becomes real. Visible. Possible.

Notice what just happened. You didn't read a program description. You didn't see admission requirements. You saw someone succeed. Someone with a name. A country. A specific, measurable impact.

That's the power of Layer Two. Achievement becomes the evidence. Success becomes the proof.

University of Alberta — Faith from Ondo State, Nigeria. Psychology graduate, photographer, community volunteer.

And Here's Where It Happens

"I know where to go."

Now watch what happens in the captions.

After showing you Olugbenga's success, the post pivots: "Discover how U of T helped Olugbenga make Reeddi happen through resources, community, and technical support."

The university isn't just a location. It's the reason the success happened.

The posts end with campus imagery. Institutional branding. Calls to action. "Do it here." "Start your journey." "Apply now."

The progression is complete:

First: Here's someone like you.

Then: Here's what they achieved.

Finally: Here's where you can do it too.

The university becomes the bridge between who you are and who you want to become.

University of Toronto — "Do it here." The institution becomes the destination. Layer Three in action.

Three layers. Every time.

This isn't random. This is strategy. This is how you make distant institutions feel accessible. How you turn application numbers into enrollment numbers.

And it works because it doesn't feel like marketing. It feels like possibility.

But here's what we need to talk about.

These stories aren't lies. Olugbenga is real. Faith is real. Their achievements are real.

But representation is selective. For every success story you see, there are stories you don't. The student who couldn't afford to stay. The visa that got denied. The graduate who moved back home because the job market didn't work out.

Instagram algorithms reward engagement. University branding prioritizes impact. So what gets amplified? The wins. The highlights. The proof that the system works.

What stays quiet? Everything else.

If you're a prospective student: These stories are powerful. Let them inspire you. But ask questions too. What's the full picture? What happens when things don't go according to plan?

If you're a university: You're not just marketing programs. You're shaping futures. The stories you choose to tell—and the ones you don't—matter more than you think.

Conclusion

Here's what I keep thinking about.

Every year, 122,000 people from West Africa apply to study in Canada. Behind each application is a late-night research session. A family conversation. A vision of the future.

And that vision? It's shaped by posts like these. By the stories universities choose to tell. By who gets to be visible and who stays in the background.

Recognition. Achievement. Direction.

That's the formula. That's how opportunity gets sold.

What you see shapes what you believe is possible. And that shaping of imagination? That might be the most powerful form of representation there is.
